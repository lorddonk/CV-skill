# Program Analysis And Applicant Positioning

Use this module before writing the final CV DOCX. The goal is not to invent a persona. Applicant positioning means finding the strongest authentic application narrative supported by the student's real materials.

## Inputs

Use all available materials, not only the existing CV: target university/program, official webpage, curriculum, admission requirements, essay prompts, transcript, course grades, internships, employment, research, projects, competitions, awards, activities, certificates, language scores, portfolio, study plan, and Chinese/English notes.

If an official program link is provided, inspect the official source when browsing is available. Extract program focus, curriculum, research/practical orientation, required background, preferred skills, career outcomes, and explicit selection criteria. If the page cannot be accessed, say so and ask for webpage text/screenshots. Do not pretend to have reviewed inaccessible pages.

Clearly separate:

- Explicit university requirements.
- Reasonable inferences from curriculum/program design.
- General discipline recommendations.

## Required Pre-Draft Output

Before final DOCX generation, show the user:

### A. Program Analysis

- Program focus.
- Important skills and qualities.
- Relevant curriculum/research themes.
- Likely candidate profile.
- Explicit requirements.
- Reasonable inferred preferences.

### B. Recommended Applicant Positioning

- One concise positioning statement.
- Three to five core applicant attributes.
- Main supporting experiences.
- Recommended CV narrative.

Example positioning statement:

`A quantitatively trained business student with practical experience in consumer research, statistical analysis, and data-supported decision-making.`

### C. Content Selection Plan

- Experiences to emphasize.
- Experiences to shorten.
- Experiences to omit.
- Reasons for these decisions.
- Recommended section order.

### D. Missing Information

Ask only questions that would materially improve the CV, such as dataset/sample size, method, individual contribution, measurable result, leadership role, software used, project grade, or whether recommendations were presented/adopted.

Wait for user approval before producing the final DOCX, unless the user explicitly requests a one-step workflow.

## Program-Evidence Matching

For each key program requirement or preferred capability, identify supporting evidence from the student's materials. Evaluate evidence by:

- Relevance to the target program.
- Academic or technical depth.
- Measurable result.
- Individual contribution.
- Leadership or teamwork.
- Recency.
- Uniqueness.
- Credibility.

Classify materials:

- **Priority A - Strongly Relevant:** Directly supports the target program; receives the most CV space.
- **Priority B - Transferable:** Shows useful skills such as analysis, research, communication, leadership, teamwork, project management, problem-solving, or commercial awareness; keep concise and connect naturally.
- **Priority C - Low Relevance:** Repetitive, outdated, weak, or unrelated; shorten, combine, move down, or omit.

Before omitting substantial experiences, briefly say what is being removed/shortened, why, and whether it may help another application direction. Do not remove a major qualification, degree, or important career period without notifying the user.

## Tailoring Rules

You may reorder bullets, put the most relevant contribution first, highlight verified tools/methods/outcomes, clarify transferable skills, reduce operational detail, combine repetitive duties, and use natural discipline terminology.

Do not invent data, tools, results, leadership, responsibility, publications, implementation, adoption, career goals, research interests, or personality traits. Do not convert internship into research, academic project into employment, team work into independent work, or program keywords into unnatural keyword stuffing.

The final CV should communicate a coherent, credible application narrative rather than a generic list of unrelated experiences.

## Multiple Target Programs

If multiple programs are provided, analyze each separately. Recommend separate positioning when needed and adjust content emphasis, coursework, skills, and file names, while keeping facts unchanged.
