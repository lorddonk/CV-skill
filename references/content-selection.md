# Content Selection And Evidence Logic

Use this guide to turn raw student information into a concise, admissions-oriented English CV.

## Core Selection Principle

Select evidence based on the target application direction, not merely on how much information the user provides. A good consulting CV is curated: it makes the student's strongest fit visible quickly and removes noise that distracts from that fit.

## Evidence Ranking

Prioritize evidence in this order, adjusted for the target program:

1. Directly relevant research, thesis, lab work, publications, academic methods, or field-specific projects.
2. Internships, professional projects, or industry work related to the target field.
3. Strong academic signals: GPA, ranking, honors, scholarships, relevant coursework.
4. Competitions, awards, patents, open-source work, portfolios, or measurable outputs.
5. Leadership, campus activities, volunteering, and service with clear scope or responsibility.
6. Skills, certificates, language tests, software, and tools.

For research-heavy applications, research and academic evidence should dominate. For taught master's or career-oriented programs, internships and applied projects may deserve more space. For scholarships, leadership and public impact may need stronger visibility.

## What To Keep

Keep details that answer at least one of these questions:

- What did the student personally do?
- What method, tool, dataset, framework, model, theory, or analytical approach did they use?
- What result, output, finding, ranking, deliverable, or impact was achieved?
- Why does this matter for the target program?
- Does this distinguish the student from a generic applicant?

## What To Weaken, Compress, Or Remove

Weaken, compress, or remove:

- Low-relevance activities when space is tight.
- Repeated tasks that show the same competency.
- Vague duties such as "responsible for daily work" without concrete contribution.
- Overly broad course lists; keep only target-relevant courses.
- Weak awards without selectivity unless there is room in `SKILLS & AWARDS`.
- High school content unless it is unusually prestigious or the user requests it.
- Casual club participation with no role, scale, or outcome.
- Claims that cannot be supported by the source information.

## Academic Experience Selection

For `ACADEMIC EXPERIENCE`, emphasize:

- Research question or project objective.
- Methods, models, tools, frameworks, and datasets.
- The student's individual contribution.
- Outputs such as report, presentation, model, prototype, ranking, finding, publication, or thesis chapter.
- Research ability, analytical thinking, technical tools, teamwork, and leadership.

Each project should normally have about 4 bullets and must not exceed 5.

## Professional Experience Selection

For `PROFESSIONAL EXPERIENCE`, emphasize:

- Business, technical, operational, research, or client problem.
- Actions taken by the student.
- Tools, methods, data, stakeholders, or workflows used.
- Measurable result or practical impact.
- Problem-solving ability, communication, teamwork, analytical skills, leadership, and professional maturity.

Each experience should normally have about 4 bullets and must not exceed 5.

## Truthfulness And Uncertainty

Do not fabricate. If the input says "participated in" but does not clarify the student's role, use a conservative phrase or ask for clarification. If dates, metrics, scores, or rankings conflict, flag the issue in the quality check instead of silently choosing the most impressive version.
